Steven Ellison made this album in his grandmother's bedroom. That fact shouldn't matter, but
it does — because 1983 sounds exactly like that: intimate, a little claustrophobic, full of
ideas bumping up against the walls of a small room and finding strange new shapes in the
corners.

The Brazilian influence hits immediately. "São Paulo" and "Orbit Brazil" are built on syncopated
rhythms that feel borrowed from a street drummer and then dissolved in something weirder —
Ellison had reportedly gotten a rhythm lesson from a Copacabana vendor that, by his own
account, "flipped his whole wig." You can hear the flip. Jazz harp, chopped drum breaks,
Afro-Cuban percussion, game-audio blips — none of it should cohere, and yet it does, because
Ellison has an ear for negative space that most producers twice his age don't.

This is not *Cosmogramma*. It's not the full vision. FlyLo has more or less disowned it, which
is his right, but it's also a little unfair to the record. What 1983 actually is: a 22-year-old
prodigy pointing at everything he loved — Dilla, Madlib, jazz, Brazil, video games — and
finding a way to make it sound like him before he even knew what that meant. That's not nothing.
That's actually rare.

"Pet Monster Shotglass" and "Shifty" are the high-water marks — dense, twitchy, and oddly
warm. "Unexpected Delight" closes things out with Laura Darlington's vocals floating over one
of the most genuinely pretty beats on the record. The weaker tracks are forgettable rather
than bad. For a debut, that's a comfortable margin.
